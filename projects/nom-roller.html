Nom Roller

Overview: Nom Roller is a rapid UX prototype designed and built for the Astra | Young Builders Symposium during Toronto Tech Week. Created under extreme time constraints, the project explores how playful interaction design can reduce decision fatigue around choosing what to eat.

My Role: Lead UX Designer — Design Direction, Interaction Design, Prototyping

Team: 4 designers

Tools: Figma, Low-Fidelity Sketching, Rapid Prototyping, Live User Testing

View Interactive Prototype →

Problem

Food decisions are deceptively complex. When users are hungry, cognitive load is high, patience is low, and too many options lead to indecision. Existing food apps optimize for exploration, not decisiveness. We saw an opportunity to design for speed, clarity, and emotional relief rather than choice abundance.

Design Approach

I set the design direction by framing the experience around a single principle: reduce thinking, increase momentum. I translated this into early low-fidelity sketches and interaction flows, then built an interactive Figma prototype to quickly align the team and test assumptions.

Key design decisions included prioritizing one primary action at a time, minimizing on-screen options, and using visual urgency cues to match the user’s hungry mental state.

Solution

The final prototype delivers randomized food suggestions through a clean, minimal interface. Bold red accents act as “hunger signals,” while large, clearly separated buttons reduce hesitation and encourage fast decisions. The experience intentionally trades depth and customization for clarity and momentum.

Validation

We tested the prototype live at the symposium using quick observational testing and short feedback forms. Users consistently understood the core interaction without explanation, validating the simplicity of the flow. Feedback highlighted moments where pacing and button hierarchy could be improved.

Iteration & Reflection

Post-testing, I led discussions around refining interaction timing, adjusting button prominence, and improving layout balance. If continued, the next iteration would focus on micro-interactions and subtle motion to further guide user attention without adding cognitive load.

Key Takeaway: This project reinforced how intentional constraint — fewer options, clearer actions, faster feedback — can dramatically improve usability, especially in high-friction decision moments.
